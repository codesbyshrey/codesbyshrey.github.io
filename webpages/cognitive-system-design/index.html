A Deeper Look at the Core Modules

M1: Sensory Processing

Design must respect the physical limits of our senses. For vision, this means ensuring high contrast and legibility, accounting for the different functions of rods (low light) and cones (color, detail).

M2: Preattentive Grouping (Gestalt)

Our brains automatically group objects before we even pay attention. Designs that violate these Gestalt principles (like Proximity) force the user's brain to work harder, increasing cognitive load.

Bad: High Load

Good: Low Load

M3: Long-Term Memory (LTM)

LTM stores our knowledge. Designs must support retrieval by providing consistent cues (Encoding Specificity) and build on our two main memory types.

M4: Metacognition

This is our "thinking about thinking." A key HF goal is to mitigate cognitive biases, like Anchoring, where an initial piece of info skews our judgment.

M5: Working Memory (WM)

WM is our limited-capacity "processor." Hick's Law proves that as you add choices (n), Reaction Time (RT) increases logarithmically. Good design simplifies.

Key Principles in Practice

Signal Detection Theory (SDT)

Is that alert a real signal or just noise? SDT helps us measure a user's ability (d') to distinguish them. A good design maximizes "Hits" and "Correct Rejections."

Hit (Signal Detected)
Miss (Signal Not Detected)
False Alarm (Noise Detected)
Correct Rejection (Noise Ignored)

Predictive Modeling (GOMS/KLM)

We can mathematically predict how long an expert user will take to complete a task. Optimizing this time by reducing steps is a core HF goal.

Cognitive Failure Modes & Solutions

When design violates these cognitive principles, performance suffers. Here are common failures and their HF-engineered solutions.

Failure: User is Overwhelmed

Cognitive Origin: M2/M5 Failure. Poor Gestalt grouping (M2) forces Working Memory (M5) to do the organizational work, causing rapid fatigue.

Solution: Apply Gestalt principles (Proximity, Similarity) to create a clear visual hierarchy that M2 can process automatically.

Failure: User Freezes or Abandons Task

Cognitive Origin: M5 Violation (Hick's Law). Excessive choice complexity (high 'n') logarithmically increases reaction time and cognitive load.

Solution: Reduce 'n'. Implement progressive disclosure and chunking to present fewer options at critical decision points.

Failure: User Can't Recall a Command

Cognitive Origin: M3 Violation (Encoding Specificity). The cues at retrieval (on the interface) do not match the cues from training.

Solution: Ensure 1:1 consistency for icons, labels, and context between the training environment and the operational system.
