Principle 1: Energy is "Quantized"

In the classical world, energy is a continuous ramp‚Äîyou can have any amount. In the quantum world, energy comes in discrete packets, or "quanta," like steps on a staircase. You can be on step 1 or step 2, but never in between.

Principle 4: Entanglement

Two particles can become "entangled," their fates linked. If you measure one, you *instantly* know the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance."

Principle 5: The Uncertainty Principle

You cannot simultaneously know certain pairs of properties, like a particle's exact position and its exact momentum, with perfect accuracy. The more you know about one, the less you can know about the other.

This chart shows the trade-off. You can have high certainty of position (but low of momentum) or vice-versa. You can never have high certainty of both (the "Forbidden Zone").

Why Does This Matter? Real-World Tech

Quantum physics isn't just theory; it's the foundation of our modern world. Many technologies we rely on only work because we understand these quantum rules.

Electronics: The transistors and semiconductors in every computer and phone are quantum devices.

Lasers: Used in everything from fiber optics to surgery, lasers rely on the quantum leap of electrons.

Medical Imaging (MRI): MRI machines use quantum "spin" to see inside the human body.

Future Computing: Quantum computers promise to solve problems impossible for today's machines by using superposition.
